# Inclusion Guidelines

Guidelines for designing inclusive apps and games that welcome everyone through respectful communication, accessible content, and diverse representation.

## Inclusive by Design

Design intuitive experiences by investigating people's goals and perspectives. Consider how people with different backgrounds might respond to your content, accounting for characteristics including:

- Age, gender/gender identity, race/ethnicity, sexuality
- Physical and cognitive attributes
- Permanent, temporary, and situational disabilities
- Language, culture, religion, education
- Social and economic context

Focus on creating a **welcoming experience**, not merely avoiding offense. An inoffensive app isn't necessarily inclusive.

## Welcoming Language

- **Tone:** Be clear, direct, and respectful. Avoid overly academic language that implies only certain education levels are welcome.
- **Address people directly:** Use *you* and *your*. Avoid *the user* or *the player*. Reserve *we* and *our* for your company/software.
- **Define specialized terms:** If technical jargon is necessary, define it and make definitions easy to find. Prefer plain language.
- **Replace colloquial expressions:** Many are culture-specific or have exclusionary origins (e.g., *peanut gallery*, *grandfathered in*). Use plain language instead.
- **Be cautious with humor:** Humor is subjective, hard to translate, and risks confusing, irritating, or insulting people.

## Being Approachable

- Present a clear, straightforward interface that doesn't require particular skills or knowledge upfront.
- Build in ways to learn — consider onboarding flows that let newcomers take a step-by-step approach while letting experienced users skip ahead.

## Gender Identity

- **Avoid unnecessary gender references.** Instead of "his or her," restructure sentences (e.g., "Subscribers can post recipes" instead of "a subscriber can post his or her recipes").
- **Use nongendered imagery.** Prefer gender-neutral avatars, emoji, glyphs, and characters. SF Symbols provides nongendered glyphs like `person.crop.circle`, `person.3.fill`, and `figure.wave`.
- **Give users customization tools** for avatars and characters rather than prescribing gender.
- **If gender data is required** (health, legal), provide inclusive options: *nonbinary*, *self-identify*, *decline to state*. Let people specify their pronouns.

## People and Settings

- **Portray human diversity** in copy, images, and related materials — varied racial backgrounds, body types, ages, and physical capabilities.
- **Avoid stereotypical representations** of occupations or behaviors (e.g., don't show only male doctors or female nurses).
- **Review settings and objects.** Avoid unnecessary displays of affluence. Prefer places, homes, activities, and items that are familiar and relatable to most people.

## Avoiding Stereotypes

- Examine unconscious biases that may influence design decisions.
- **Don't assume narrow definitions** (e.g., "family" meaning only a woman, man, and biological children).
- **Avoid culture-specific assumptions** in features like security questions. Replace questions like "What was your favorite subject in college?" or "What was the make of your first car?" with universal experiences:
  - What's your favorite activity?
  - What was the name of your first friend?
  - What quality describes you best?

## Accessibility

- Support Apple accessibility features: VoiceOver, Display Accommodations, closed captioning, Switch Control, Speak Screen.
- **Don't assume disabilities prevent interest** in your app or game.
- Remember each disability is a **spectrum**, and everyone can experience **temporary** (e.g., infection-caused hearing loss) or **situational** disabilities (e.g., can't hear on noisy train).
- Include people with disabilities when representing diverse people.
- Avoid language that uses disability to express negative qualities.
- Take a **people-first approach** when writing about disabilities.
- **Prioritize simplicity and perceivability** — ensure content is perceivable via sight, hearing, or touch.

## Languages and Localization

- **Internationalize** your app to handle multiple languages and regions.
- Inclusive practices (plain language, no unnecessary gender references, diverse representation, avoiding stereotypes) naturally prepare content for localization.
- Use **SF Symbols** for glyphs — they include language-specific and bidirectional variants.
- **Be aware of cultural color meanings.** Colors carry strong culture-specific associations (e.g., white = death in some cultures, purity in others). Verify your color choices communicate the intended meaning in each locale.
